# Nomenclature of organic chemistry

The [[@favreNomenclatureOrganicChemistry2013|Blue Book]] describes a (semi)systematic nomenclature designed so that, loosely speaking, the correspondence between compound names and molecular structures is an [[Surjectivity, injectivity, and bijectivity|injection]].
The resulting unique compound names, called <dfn id="PIN">preferred IUPAC names</dfn> or <abbr title="preferred IUPAC names">PINs</abbr>, do not supplant traditional names, but rather supplement them.[^P-10]

This PKM is not intended to give a complete description of this nomenclature, for that see the aforementioned [[@favreNomenclatureOrganicChemistry2013|Blue Book]].

## Substitutive nomenclature

The basic idea of <dfn>substitutive nomenclature</dfn> is that we have a [[Organic parent structure|parent structure]] (usually [[Parent hydride|parent hydride]], and more often than not a carbon hydride),
upon which we apply formal [[Operations of organic nomenclature|operations]].
These operations are necessarily bidirectional:

1. (**forward**) To go from name to structure, we apply operations indicated by affixes/infixes to the parent hydride in the opposite direction.
2. (**reverse**) To go from structure to name, we apply operations in one direction to reach a bare hydride (the parent hydride)

The eponymous, and most ubiquitous formal operation is [[Operations of organic nomenclature#Subtitutive operation|substitution]], in the forward direction replacing some number of hydrogen atoms with a [[Organic group|group]].[^P-12.1]
